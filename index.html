Ted Kaehler introduced the metaphor into the digital realm with the BitRectEditor for Smalltalk in 1975, allowing users to select one option from a set. For some time most designs included little circles to select one option, as most have experienced in a Google Form. Now we see how radio buttons designers use icons and different sizes to create intuitive & accessible designs.

This exploration will look at how Pinterest, Duolingo, and Apple’s Settings have all tailored the design of radio buttons for different interaction purposes.

Pinterest

The Use of Original Radio Buttons

Pinterest does not use many radio buttons, their designs mainly include dropdowns and multi-selects. I have chosen to analyze this one use of radio buttons to understand why it is the only time this component is implemented in core design of their User Interface.

Duolingo

The Use of Radio Buttons with Numbering and Tag Containers

Duolingo uses many different forms of radio buttons, including those with easy-to-understand icons as tag options, as well as numbering the available options to users. In this case I have decided to analyze one form of their many radio button designs where the buttons themselves are numbers, and how this affects learnability and accessibility.

Apple Settings

Challenging what a Radio Button looks like with Colorful Tag Options

Finally I chose Apple's Desktop Settings, as the design challenges what a traditional radio button can look like the most out of all three applications. The classic radio button is used, as seen in my annotations. However there are other designs mixed in including previews of what pressing each radio button option will result in, as well as making the button itself correspond to the value users are selecting (color buttons).

Comparing Outputs of all Three Implementations

After annotating some key observations from the design implementation of radio buttons for each user interface, I wanted to understand what the key interaction differences were for each application for mousepad, keyboard, and, mobile/touch users. I focused on how good and sometimes lacking implementations also affected learnability, memorability, efficiency, and accessibility.

  • Upon searching a keyword using the search bar, a series of filters appears at the top of the search results. One clicks on the “Filter” tool, which makes a radio button selection appear.
  • You can click on every content option by either using the radio button itself on the right hand side. The clickable target area is larger including the options’ words (e.g. Videos) which makes it easier for users to interact with. The chosen option’s word also becomes bolded.
  • The radio button filter is only applied once users hit “Apply” at the bottom of the side menu. This is not intuitive at first since this button appears to be separate from the radio button selection, as visually it is much further down the sidebar menu.
  • In the initial state, none of the buttons are selected.
  • When hovering between options, users don’t have to hover over the number, rather the entire container option works. The container is much larger than the numbers & each label tag making it more accessible to different mouse users.
  • When clicking on a button, the drop shadow disappears until the click is released. This makes the container bounce when clicked on, adding an additional queue to users.
  • Once one option is selected, users have no way of clicking away back to the initial state. This increases learnability as it’s signaled to users they must submit one of the radio button options.
  • In the initial state, one of the settings is already selected. This increases learnability as users are automatically able to grasp that they must select one of the options. Having a default selected option can help.
  • For the color selection, the color of the button itself aligns with the selection increasing memorability of the component.
  • All the radio buttons have default choices set, which helps users understand that they can select the other options, by clicking over the image.
  • The classic looking radio buttons are the hardest to click on because of small text size and button size.
Keyboard
  • F + Enter – to access the pop-up radio buttons in the first place. Then pressing the Pageup & Pagedown keys makes users scroll through the image gallery on the right rather than the filters themselves.
  • This component is not accessible as the keyboard only works in conjunction with the mouse
  • Hitting the esc key does not clear the selected option or go to the clear all button at the top of the radio buttons
  • Choosing between the radio buttons with Pageup and Pagedown keys only works when the radio buttons are activated first with a mouse press. Choosing between buttons works but submitting the filter with the Enter key does not work.
  • As a standalone feature it misses the connection between opening the radio buttons, and then being able to use them. This majorly affects accessibility for these types of users.
  • Click on the number in the keyboard to choose between the number on the list, makes this perhaps more accessible than just having the dots/targets in a classic radio button selection.
  • Hitting the esc key does not clear the selected option or go to the clear all button at the top of the radio buttons.
  • Not possible to deselect an option once selected (1,2, or 3). You cannot deselect one component – once you select a component you can switch to another option but you cannot fully deselect
  • No hovering over feature available with the keyboard version, rather clicking on numbers selects the option for you.
  • Does not work with Pageup and Pagedown keys to select options, as well as awsd options – not necessarily intuitive that one should be pressing 123
  • Pageup/down keys do become activated after users start the selection by pressing either 1/2/3 as an option – then users can select using either key group.
  • There is minimal keyboard navigation available which majorly hinders accessibility.
  • Any keyboard actions only work for navigating through the sidebar menu of Apple Settings rather than the page with the different radio buttons themselves.
  • Even upon initializing the interaction with a set of radio buttons using a mouse, it did not work continuing to navigate using Pageup/Pagedown or numbers.
Touch (Mobile Version)
  • The total clickable area extends to the button itself and the label tag, like in the desktop version
  • The label tag is not bolded when selected, unlike the desktop version. This inconsistency affects memorability for users between the desktop vs mobile version.
  • The size of the button targets themselves is smaller on the mobile version.
  • Everytime the radio button selection is submitted, the radio pop-up disappears and the search results are filtered. This affects the efficiency, as this includes steps in the workflow when users want to toggle between labels.
  • The interaction lacks intuition as every time users select which radio button they are interested in, the radio button popup disappears and filters the results.
  • The mobile version does not have the text bolded of the selected label tag the way the desktop version does, which decreases accessibility for mobile users.
  • There is no exact mobile version of the “Appearance” tab on Apple’s Mobile settings, though there are some other uses of radio buttons.
  • The clickable area for all radio buttons is large making it accessible for different users.
  • Most of the mobile radio buttons also use previews that can also be clicked on, which makes it very intuitive for users to understand what they are selecting.

State Models with Intentional Trade-offs

I now revised my state models, making an intentional trade-off that affects how we value the components' learnability, memorability, efficiency, and accessibility. This could mean making the component more usable for screen reader and keyboard users rather than for repeated mouse users, which is a design trade-off for those users. The main change is that when keyboard users want to escape the selection they may do so by hitting the escape button. This does however mean that whenever mouse users stop their selection and click elsewhere on the screen, the selection will refresh and they will have to restart choosing which radio button.

Redesigning a Component

Initial State Redesign: Duolingo

This redesign of the initial state helps users understand that a selection is required with radio buttons. The design should also signal that these options/tags are mutually exclusive, meaning that they must click between these.

Having one preselected option may help first-time users understand that one needs to be selected. The additional proposal of having the buttons rotate in being highlighted helps learnability and memorability. First-time users can understand that they can toggle between options as much as they like. For increasing accessibility, when changing between highlighting button options, the keyboard keys to do so could be displayed on the screen.

Additional State Re-Design: Pinterest Explicit Clickable Target

This redesign of the interaction with the selected (active) button enhances efficiency and learnability, as there were some flaws in the design noted in my annotations.

I eliminated an unneccessary workflow with the "Clear All" button. Clicking on this button would bring users back to the "All Pins" button within the radio selection. The design can simply have the "All Pins" without this additional button, as clearing does not actually clear the selection it just brings users back to the default option. As for learnability, using a border like Duolingo to signify the total clickable area makes it more obvious which button is selected and where mouse users can click.

Reflection

Watching Kat Holmes’ Talk on Rethinking Inclusive Design informed how I reflect on the outcome of this project to answer the following questions:

Key Guiding Questions:
  • Where did the components you observed do well in terms of usability and accessibility? How have you applied this to your design?
  • Are there accessibility considerations that were not addressed that you accounted for in your own design?
  • How does the change you’ve made in your own design solve for a “mismatch” in our world?

    • Some key takeaways include: The components observed across Duolingo, Pinterest, and Apple’s Settings had different strengths in usability and accessibility. For example, Pinterest’s radio buttons extended the clickable area beyond just the button, including the text label, making selection easier. Duolingo provided a large clickable area and better keyboard navigation compared to the other two implementations. I applied Duolingo's boxed clickable areas to redesigning Pinterest's interaction. Apple Settings was the most creative with their interpretations of radio buttons. Their designs also set default states signify to new users that a mutually exclusive selection must be made.
    There were many accessibility concerns that were not addressed in my design exploration. In the case of Apple Settings for instance, most of the keyboard functionalities were lacking. This redesign would include redesigning multiple interactions at once as I was looking at a page with multiple different radio button types used. Most importantly, as we saw with the trade-off exercise, it's hard to address some design elements that make it easier for keyboard users, but then change mouse functionalities.
    Kat Holmes’ concept of "mismatch" highlights how design choices can exclude users with different abilities. A major mismatch I identified was the lack of a way to deselect a previously selected option in most designs. This limitation forced users to always have a selection, which might not align with all use cases. My design includes an explicit way to "clear selection" in the trade-off section to allow users more flexibility.

    Going back my exploration, I want to give two examples of how the accessibility/inaccessibility of those inputs or outputs could also impact users with impairments either positively or negatively. The major accessibility issue observed was limited keyboard navigation. Many of the tested interfaces did not allow full navigation via keyboard alone, making them inaccessible for users who rely on assistive technologies. In my design, I ensured that users could navigate the radio buttons using only the keyboard (arrow keys, Enter key for selection, and Esc to clear the selection if applicable). When evaluating interaction costs, the size of the target is crucial. Since radio buttons are naturally small, they can be difficult to click or tap. Expanding the target area improves usability, making selection easier. Users should be able to select an option not only by clicking the circle but also by clicking the associated label.

    This exploration has taught me that mouse users are of top priority in the process of designing components. This has clearly been displayed in the case of radio buttons, where it's hard to transfer that same level of intiution for first time users and maintain learnability for keyboard users for instance. Touch and mobile is easier to adjust for a component like Radio Buttons. It's hard to transfer the mutually exlusive nature of radio buttons to screenreaders for instance, as they may only read each word individually rather than as a list of options. This has a major impact on users outside of the mouse interaction.
